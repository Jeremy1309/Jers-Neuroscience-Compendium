Neurons & Neural Signaling

Neurons communicate through both electrical signaling and chemical signaling. Action potentials carry information down the axon, while neurotransmitters carry that information across synapses to the next cell.

Action Potentials — Electrical Communication

An action potential is a rapid electrical signal that allows neurons to communicate with one another. It is generated by a temporary change in the electrical charge across the neuron's membrane and travels down the axon, carrying information to the synaptic boutons at the axon's ends.

Neurons maintain a voltage difference between the exterior and interior of the cell, called the resting membrane potential; this interior voltage level is -70 mV relative to the exterior. We rely on sodium-potassium pumps, located on the cell membrane, to keep the inside of the cell negatively charged relative to the outside. These pumps maintain this balance by transporting 3 sodium ions out of the cell for every 2 potassium ions brought in, creating a net negative charge inside the neuron.

The membrane potential changes as a result of hyperpolarizations (membrane potential becomes more negative, usually through Cl-) and depolarizations (membrane potential becomes more positive, usually through Na+). Hyperpolarizations occur through inhibitory postsynaptic potentials (IPSPs), while depolarizations occur through excitatory postsynaptic potentials (EPSPs).

Once the interior reaches the threshold potential (-50 mV), through EPSPs, no further stimulation is required and depolarization continues until the inside of the cell reaches +30 mV, relative to the outside. Once +30 mV is achieved, an action potential is triggered, and the cell returns to the resting membrane potential (-70 mV), but briefly overcompensates and reaches roughly -80 mV before returning to -70 mV.

Once an action potential is triggered, the cell enters an absolute refractory period, which is a short period during which a second action potential cannot be triggered. This is immediately followed by a short relative refractory period, where another action potential can be triggered but will require a greater stimulus than before. Once these two short periods are finished, the cell returns to its resting membrane potential and is ready to trigger another action potential.

Neurotransmitters — Chemical Communication

When an action potential reaches the axon terminal, it depolarizes the membrane, opening voltage-gated Ca2+ channels. The resulting influx of Ca2+ triggers vesicle fusion with the presynaptic membrane by binding to synaptotagmin, a Ca2+-sensing protein on synaptic vesicles. This leads to neurotransmitter release into the synaptic cleft via exocytosis.

The neurotransmitters then bind to receptors on the postsynaptic neuron, causing ion channels to open and producing either excitatory (depolarizing) or inhibitory (hyperpolarizing) effects.

The decision of whether a neuron fires its own action potential depends on the integration of incoming signals. Neurons receive input from many presynaptic cells at once, and this input is summed in two ways:

  • Spatial summation: Signals from multiple synapses across the dendrites and soma combine to influence the overall membrane potential.
  • Temporal summation: Repeated signals from the same synapse, arriving in rapid succession, can accumulate and amplify their effect.

If the sum of these excitatory and inhibitory signals causes the membrane potential at the axon hillock to reach the threshold, the neuron will fire an action potential. Conversely, if inhibitory signals outweigh excitatory ones, the neuron becomes less likely to fire, suppressing its activity.

Before being released into the synapse, neurotransmitters are held inside vesicles, sac-like structures in the presynaptic neuron. Once an action potential reaches the end of the neuron, vesicles merge with the presynaptic wall and release neurotransmitters into the synapse (synaptic cleft), where they can then bind to and influence the postsynaptic neuron.

There are many different types of neurotransmitters, and each affects the brain and body in its own way. The type of neurotransmitter a neuron releases depends on the kind of neuron: each will typically release one main type, such as a dopaminergic neuron releasing dopamine or a glutamatergic neuron releasing glutamate.

  • Glutamate: The brain's main excitatory neurotransmitter; plays a key role in learning and memory.
  • GABA: The main inhibitory neurotransmitter; helps calm brain activity and reduce anxiety.
  • Dopamine: Involved in reward, motivation, pleasure, and motor control; imbalances are linked to conditions like Parkinson's and addiction.
  • Serotonin: Helps regulate mood, sleep, appetite, and digestion; often targeted by antidepressants.
  • Acetylcholine: Supports muscle activation, attention, and learning; important in both the brain and peripheral nervous system.
  • Norepinephrine (noradrenaline): Increases alertness, focus, and arousal; part of the body's stress response.
Long-Term Potentiation

Long-term potentiation (LTP) is a long-lasting increase in synaptic strength that occurs following repeated or strong stimulation, and it is most commonly studied at glutamatergic synapses.

Note: glutamate is not always released; instead, it is released only when an action potential reaches the presynaptic terminal of a glutamatergic neuron, triggering Ca2+-dependent vesicle fusion and neurotransmitter release.

Once released, glutamate binds to both AMPA and NMDA receptors on the postsynaptic neuron. AMPA receptors are widely expressed on many neurons that receive excitatory input, though not all cells have them, and their activation allows Na+ to enter the cell, causing depolarization. This depolarization is essential because it removes the Mg2+ block from NMDA receptors, which only open when both glutamate is bound and the postsynaptic neuron is sufficiently depolarized.

When NMDA receptors open, Ca2+ enters the postsynaptic neuron and acts as a second messenger, activating intracellular signaling pathways such as phosphorylation cascades. These processes lead to the insertion of additional AMPA receptors into the postsynaptic membrane and can also induce structural changes at the synapse. As a result, future glutamate release produces a larger postsynaptic response, strengthening the synapse.

Glial Cells

Glial cells are non-neuronal cells in the brain that provide crucial support to neurons. Unlike neurons, which transmit electrical signals, glial cells play several important roles, including nourishing and protecting neurons, maintaining the blood-brain barrier, removing waste, and helping in signal transmission. They are essentially neuron parents.

There are different subtypes:

  • Ependymal: Produce CSF (cerebral spinal fluid).
  • Astrocytes: Remove excess neurotransmitters, provide structural support, and regulate the blood-brain barrier.
  • Microglia: Immune function.
  • Oligodendroglia: Insulate axons in the CNS.
  • Schwann cells: Insulate axons in the PNS.
Neuroplasticity & Neurodegeneration

Neuroplasticity is the brain's ability to adapt and reorganize by forming new neural connections. It underlies learning, memory, and recovery after injury, allowing the brain to compensate for damage or adapt to new experiences. This involves strengthening or weakening synaptic connections (synaptic plasticity) and can occur throughout life, though it is most active during development.

In contrast, neurodegeneration refers to the progressive loss of neurons and their functions. It is seen in diseases like Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, and ALS, where critical brain regions deteriorate over time, impairing memory, movement, or cognition. While neuroplasticity offers potential for recovery, severe or widespread degeneration can overwhelm the brain's capacity to adapt.
